Horror is, without a doubt, my favourite genre. I find myself, therefore, deeply disappointed when I sit down to watch a horror film and leave unmoved - a fate that befalls me more often than I would like, and one I was quietly bracing for going into _Obsession_; the hype surrounding the movie had reached a pitch that, in my experience, tends to precede a let-down. Notwithstanding, within the first thirty minutes, these concerns were entirely put to rest. _Obsession_ stands out as an expertly directed film - a masterclass, I would argue, in building genuine dread.

As someone who has consumed a wealth of horror-related media, I have long argued that tension is the most effective device for making a movie scary. Many contemporary horror films, particularly in the supernatural genre, rely far too heavily on cheap jump scares. However, things are often far scarier in our minds than they are on screen, which is precisely why the most effective horror media I have encountered places tension at the forefront. Although _Obsession_ is not without its share of traditional jump scares - effective ones, to its credit - the film truly flourishes in the spaces between them, enabled in equal parts by Curry Barker's phenomenal direction and what I can only describe as a generational performance from Inde Navarrette.

What compelled me to write this piece is that, beneath the facade of a supernatural horror movie, Obsession is a deeply human narrative rooted in the horrifying reality of what happens when someone's entitlement goes unaddressed, and the effect that it can have on another individual's autonomy. There are - I would argue - three distinct facets to the movie, and running beneath all three is the uncomfortable suggestion that the monster at the centre of this story may not be the one we initially assume it to be.

# Chapter I: Control

_For the purpose of clarity, the entity inhabiting Nicky's body will hereafter be referred to as "Freaky Nicky"._

Control is one of three defining themes of _Obsession_, and its most visceral manifestation is Freaky Nicky literally taking control over Nicky’s body. From the moment Bear makes the wish until the end of the movie, it's clear that Nicky’s body has been taken over by an external force, and no single scene in the film communicates this more effectively than Freaky Nicky’s creepy backwards walk. It is immediately unsettling; reminiscent, in its uncanniness, of Persephone from Disney's earliest and most faltering attempt at realistic human animation.

The moment Freaky Nicky registers that it has upset Bear, it appears to “rewind" its own actions; like a backtracking algorithm, it attempts to physically reverse to a previous state. It is a haunting piece of visual design, used as a vehicle to remind us - once again - that Freaky Nicky isn’t something human. And this uncaniness is not just by coincidence - rather, it’s by design.

My favourite scene in the film is when Bear wakes up at night to find Freaky Nicky standing in the corner of the room, wailing. There is a deep, instinctive sense that something is profoundly wrong with her appearance, even if you cannot immediately articulate what; in the low light, her face subtly appears to melt and morph before our very eyes.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/2.jpg"
  alt="Freaky Nicky crying in a dark room, her face appearing inhuman and distorted."
  caption="Freaky Nicky wailing in the dark."
/>

The film uses the uncanny valley as a vehicle to invoke a deep, primal sense of fear by reminding us that this _thing_ that we are looking at is something deeply inhuman. Human beings are, by necessity, attuned to the faces of other human beings. We have spent our entire evolutionary history reading them - for threat, for intention, for emotional cues. When something presents as almost human but falls just short of it, that finely-tuned recognition system misfires and begins sending signals that something is wrong.

What makes Barker's execution of this scene so impressive is that he achieves it without a frame of CGI. The effect is produced entirely through hyper-specific, uncanny makeup, engineered to exploit a genuine psychological phenomenon known as the Troxler Effect. When the eyes fix on a single point in dim lighting, the brain, bored of processing the same stagnant visual data, begins to omit details. In its desperation to fill the gaps, it hallucinates, warping the familiar geometry of a human face into something monstrous.

That scene has a particular resonance for me, because it is the same science behind the Bloody Mary urban legend - one particularly popular growing up in England. The ritual, for the uninitiated: walk alone into a pitch-black bathroom, armed with nothing but a candle, repeat her name three times into the mirror, and eventually your reflection will morph into that of Bloody Mary. The makeup used in the bedroom scene is designed to trigger this same optical illusion. Upon reflection after my first watch, I found myself struck by just how extraordinary that is as a filmmaking choice. There is something almost perversely fitting about a film so preoccupied with the loss of control deploying, as its most potent weapon, a technique that hijacks your own mind against you. By manipulating the literal physiology of our eyes, Barker forces us to share Bear's perspective entirely. We are not merely watching something take control of Nicky. We are losing control of our own senses.

That visual distortion is, however, only the outward symptom of a far more calculated psychological invasion. Freaky Nicky, whatever it is, cannot grasp something as abstract as love. Instead, it identifies what Bear values most and attempts to replicate those things as a mechanism of emotional control. We see this surface-level assimilation in the film's climax, when it dresses itself as Sarah, operating on the logic that this will "make her look pretty again." But the most macabre expression of this same rigid thinking concerns Bear's late cat, Sandy.

It begins with a subtle, telling error during Freaky Nicky’s first interaction with Bear:

Here, Freaky Nicky completely betrays its own strategy. It glitches, like an LLM trying to process a prompt - it isn’t even sure what it’s trying to achieve yet.

The script makes an effort to highlight just how uncanny this sounds.

<Script>
  <Action>
    The repeated “yeah” in the same tone is eerie and makes Bear a bit wary.
  </Action>

Freaky Nikki, whatever inhuman entity it may be, lacks any genuine sense of empathy. And in a contemporary society, we see this kind of automated empathy all the time, think of the generic corporate condolences sent after a tragedy, or a chatbot programmed to spit out platitudes when a user triggers a 'sad' keyword. It’s an imitation of care designed purely to placate and manage a situation. Freaky Nikki’s slow, repeated “yeah” comes across as if it’s an AI, figuring out the right approach and affirming to itself “yeah, this works”. Much like how it tries to physically backtrack later in the movie, this moment highlights that Freaky Nicky is, from the very beginning, calculated to the point of being algorithmic.

This machine-like calibration is also seen in how, Freaky Nicky fabricates a story about Nicky's father having cancer. Once it identifies grief as a reliable emotional anchor, Freaky Nicky begins to deploy that anchor in increasingly disturbing ways. What follows is a descent into a kind of horrifying literalism. Sandy's memory becomes a resource to be exploited through a rigid, algorithmic formula that never varies. It escalates when Freaky Nicky feeds Bear a sandwich containing Sandy's remains, layering that same rigid equation with a second variable: Bear’s ambition of becoming a food critic.

As Freaky Nicky’s grip on reality begins to deteriorate in the film's final act, this logic starts surfacing almost involuntarily. When Bear enters the house, Freaky Nicky moves through the shadows with something approaching a cat-like gait. It's easy to miss on a first watch, but it's the epitome of how the entity tries to establish emotional control over Bear: a haunting amalgamation of Sarah, Nicky, and Sandy.

Yet Freaky Nicky is not the only presence in this film attempting to exert control over Bear. The supernatural horror of the entity is mirrored, by the human, toxic reality of Bear's social circle.

Ian is, in some respects, the most straightforwardly readable. The film leaves his history with Nicky somewhat ambiguous, but the script does not. We get hints like this one, during Nicky's toast:

<Script>
  <Character
    name="NICKY"
    line="And everyone in this room will die... (looking at Ian) never feeling the strength of connection I have felt with my Bear."
  />
</Script>

The script notes that Ian shoots her an almost hurt look, making it abundantly clear that the two were involved in some capacity, and - moreover - that Ian harbours feelings towards Nicky of some sort.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/7.jpg"
  alt="Nicky standing above Ian at the party, looking down at him as the room watches on."
  caption="As Freaky Nicky looks down at Ian, almost as if the words were directed at him, he glances at the floor with a melancholy look in his eyes."
/>

Viewed through that lens, what reads in the film as brotherly ribbing reveals itself as something considerably more deliberate. Not only does he encourage Bear to call Nicky by a nickname she visibly despises, but he also advises him that confessing his feelings would cost Bear any relationship with her. The script notes that Bear is openly confused by this advice, because it makes no sense as genuine counsel; it only makes sense as sabotage. Moreover, when Bear later tells Ian about Nicky's father, the script describes Ian as looking shocked in a way that implies prior knowledge. He withholds, much as he hides the truth about himself and Nicky.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/8.jpg"
  alt="Ian's face registering a flash of shock upon hearing news about Nicky's father."
  caption="Ian's reaction upon hearing about Nicky's father, a flash of shock that implies he knows more than he is letting on."
/>

He is not a better friend to Sarah, either; he discloses to Bear that Nicky had told Sarah that she "sees him like a brother" - something which Nicky had told Sarah in confidence. Notwithstanding, Sarah's motivations are no less self-interested. When she is cut off before she can tell Bear about Ian and Nicky, the context is revealing: she had just learned that Ian had been spreading her private business. She was not going to tell Bear out of loyalty. Rather, she intended on telling him out of retaliation.

<Script>
  <Character
    name="SARAH"
    line="And also if Ian’s gonna spread my shit, then you should know Ian and Nicky have–"
  />
</Script>

Which prompts a reassessment of the scene in her car where she does eventually Bear about Ian and Nicky hooking up. She does not lead with the fling. Rather, she circles it, instead raising her concerns about the dynamic between Nicky and Bear by suggesting that Nicky is taking advantage of him. Bear pushes back, and the script tells us plainly: _Sarah's eyes have a secret_. Moreover, just as Ian shared something Nicky had told Sarah in confidence, Sarah prefaces her own revelation with _"I promised Ian that I wouldn't tell you this"_, a throwaway line which exposes the web of broken confidences running through the entire friend group.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/9.jpg"
  alt="Sarah looking down, her expression guarded, as though holding something back."
  caption="Sarah circles the truth rather than stating it outright, looking down as she contemplates whether to tell Bear or not."

Withheld information, manufactured doubt, and sabotage framed as advice - this is a friend group that is, in its own thoroughly human way, entirely fluent in control. Notwithstanding, beneath Freaky Nicky, beneath Ian's lies, beneath Sarah's secrets, there is a far more troubling example of control playing out; the control that Bear establishes over Nicky.

# Chapter II: Autonomy

_Obsession_ is a movie which lends itself well to a rewatch. Details that once felt like throwaway lines reveal themselves, in retrospect, as quiet confessions the film was making all along. Chief among these is the realisation, after a first viewing, that the real Nicky never left. The script corroborates this - noting, during the party scene, a brief moment in which _“the TRUE NICKY emerges”_. As soon as Bear makes the wish at the beginning of the film, the real Nicky is nothing more than a puppet, stripped of her autonomy and agency. And so, when rewatching, a deeply unsettling realisation slowly takes hold: beneath the facade of Freaky Nicky, the real Nicky is still there - entirely powerless, and completely trapped.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/10.jpg"
  alt="Nicky sitting alone in the diner, an ordinary moment that takes on a darker weight on a second viewing."
  caption="With the knowledge of an initial watch, every shot of Nicky becomes ambiguous. Are we looking at Freaky Nicky, or the real one, still trapped by the One Wish Willow?"
/>

This lack of autonomy is a central facet of _Obsession_’s horror. The movie deals with an extremely horrible topic with a degree of maturity. It isn’t gratuitous, rather, it treats Nicky’s powerlessness as nothing less than deeply horrifying. We realise the gravity of this situation in the short snippets we receive of the real Nicky emerging. There are a myriad of obvious instances in the theatrical cut, each growing in increasing panic and franticness, with Bear’s reactions to these snippets comparatively becoming more and more desensitised. It’s a juxtaposition which aims to emphasise just how much Bear becomes irredeemable through his lack of concern for Nicky’s autonomy.

The first real instance in the movie is when Nicky and Bear are making out for the first time and we get our first real jumpscare of the movie which, I will admit, got me the first time I watched it. When Nicky regains her autonomy for a brief, fleeting moment and finds herself making out with Bear she is **absolutely terrified**. Evidently, she does not want to be in that situation. However, I think Bear’s immediate reaction to this is interesting. _“You made me think I did something you didn’t like”_. He’s not so much concerned about her, as much as he is that he did something out of line.

Contrarily, the real Nicky’s opinions on the matter are made abundantly clear. In the following interaction between the two when Nicky stands at Bear’s door in her red dress, there is a blink-and-you’ll miss it moment:

<Script>
  <Character name="NICKY" line="I'm not saying you took advantage of me." />
  <Character
    name="BEAR"
    line="Nicky, I didn't know you were taking anything."
  />
  <Character name="NICKY" line="That’s what I’m saying, I’m not saying that." />
  <Character name="BEAR" line="Not saying what?" />
  <Character name="NICKY" line="You took advantage of me!" />
  <Character name="BEAR" line="I don't think... I didn't" />
  <Character name="NICKY" line="I know that’s why... I’m saying you didn’t." />
</Script>

Her cadence when she says _"you took advantage of me"_ isn't an answer, it's a statement. That is not Freaky Nicky speaking; that is the real Nicky, surfacing briefly to articulate, with unmistakable clarity, exactly how she feels about what transpired the previous night. This makes the slight confusion that follows, as Freaky Nicky reasserts itself and insists that Bear did no such thing, all the more telling.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/11.jpg"
  alt="Freaky Nicky standing at the door in a red dress, a brief glint in her eye hinting at something beneath the surface."
  caption="A fleeting moment where the real Nicky seems to surface; the glint in Freaky Nicky's eye suggests that there is something beneath the surface."
/>

Notwithstanding, supernatural phenomena, esotericism, and one-wish-willow weirdness aside - it’s worth examining the situation from Bear’s perspective alone. Although he claims to not have known that she was on something, as far as he knows, she was clearly drunk. She tells Bear in the car that _“I must be pretty fucked up”_, to which his immediate response is _“yes, I know”_. In spite of this, he makes no attempt to stop her advances. Hence why, as Nicky so clearly puts it, _“you took advantage of me”_. The significance of that line, however, extends beyond the moment itself. It is the first suggestion we receive that Nicky's autonomy was never fully extinguished, but merely suppressed. Which, in many ways, is a far more horrifying proposition.

In the scene where Freaky Nicky stands in the corner watching Bear sleep, she tells Bear that _“I don’t like my dreams. I feel like you don’t love me as much as I do. Like it’s not mutual.”_ Freaky Nicky is right that the feeling is not mutual, though not in the way it thinks. It is not Bear's feelings for Nicky that are in question here; it is the real Nicky's feelings for Bear. Sleep, it would seem, returns to Nicky some measure of her latent autonomy, and Freaky Nicky is afraid of what she does with it. Later in the film, we hear the real Nicky tell Bear:

<Script>
  <Character name="NICKY" line="She's sleeping. It's me." />
  <Character name="BEAR" line="You are you Nicky." />
  <Character name="NICKY" line="Shhhh. Please don’t wake her up." />
</Script>

It is the same reason she was able to write _"NOT ME"_ on the polaroid placed in Bear's lunchbox - a desperate attempt to tell him that the woman he is in a relationship with is not her. Throughout the entirety of the film, Nicky is forced to watch her own life play out from behind her own eyes, a passenger in her own body, unable to intervene.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/12.jpg"
  alt="A polaroid photograph of Bear and Nicky with 'NOT ME' written along the bottom in handwriting."
  caption="A message from the real Nicky, left in Bear's lunchbox."
/>

And if that were not horrifying enough, beyond her autonomy, she is made to lose her dignity as well. When Bear comes home to find her standing in a pool of her own bodily fluids, Nicky is trapped behind that too - acutely aware, acutely humiliated, and entirely without recourse. As Freaky Nicky stands at the door beaming, the juxtaposition to how the real Nicky feels could not be starker; when Bear calls the number on the One Wish Willow box and the man on the other end asks if he would like to speak to Nicky, she is **screaming her lungs out**.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/13.jpg"
  alt="Freaky Nicky standing at the door, beaming with a wide, unmoving smile."
  caption="Freaky Nicky's unwavering smile - a stark contrast to the agony that lies beneath it."
/>

The tragic culmination of all of this arrives at Ian's party, where Nicky - in the fleeting moment of autonomy she has managed to reclaim - attempts to escape the only way she believes she can.

<Script>
  <Action>
    She moves abruptly, franticly searching for ANYTHING that could hurt her.
  </Action>
</Script>

When this doesn’t work, in the last long interaction between Bear and the real Nicky in the movie, she gives him one, final, pleading request: _“kill me please”_. However, by this point, Bear is far beyond redemption. In the scene right before this, it’s clear that he’s already come to the realisation that the thing inhabiting Nicky’s body isn’t Nicky. But instead of showing any semblance of care or concern for her, he simply pleads and asks her to _“God, just be like Nicky!”_.

In his mind, he has everything that he always wanted. The real Nicky has been entirely dehumanised and now represents this idealistic relationship which he believes that he is entitled to. And so, when Nicky pleads with him to give her a way out, his response is completely devoid of any empathy for her - instead, missing the point entirely, and asking _“what’s so bad about being with me?”_. To which Nicky makes it clear to him _“I’ve never been with you Bear”_.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/14.jpg"
  alt="Bear's face falling into sadness after hearing Nicky's plea."
  caption="Bear's face falling into sadness after hearing Nicky's plea, but not for her - his unapologetic selfishness prevents him from having any empathy for Nicky."
/>

Even after hearing her scream on the phone, after seeing her recurrent beats of panic, even after hearing Nicky beg for him to kill her and tell him that she was never with him - he still doesn’t care. And as her cries grow in increasing sadness, he turns his back to the woman he claims to love and walks out. The script captures this with a haunting note that is nothing less than heartbreaking.

<Script>
  <Action>
    Bear contemplates his actions. Slowly, he turns and exits the room. Despite
    her pleas, Bear shows no empathy.
  </Action>
</Script>

The most frightening aspect of _Obsession_ is the manner in which Nicky is robbed of her autonomy, and the film makes clear, in no uncertain terms, that the true culprit is not Freaky Nicky, but Bear. What is equally horrifying, however, is his reaction to all of this. Or rather, his absence of one. He exhibits no semblance of a normal human response, because what he feels towards her was never love, or care; it was obsession.

# Chapter III: _Obsession_

It feels as though, in a contemporary film landscape, one of the worst things that a movie can be is “predictable”. Naturally, when a movie diverts audience expectations, it can lend itself to a more memorable viewing experience. In my opinion, the biggest twist in _Obsession_ is its title. For the majority of the runtime, we believe we are watching Nicky's obsession with Bear. However, we are watching Bear's obsession with her.

As the movie progresses, Bear becomes more and more desensitised at Nicky's behaviour - eventually reaching a point of complete apathy. When Nicky builds a literal shrine to Sandy right at the beginning of the movie, he doesn’t flag this as a massive psychological break at all. Rather, he simply tells her how messed up it is, and just moves on. And this lack of any perceivable empathy or concern is present throughout the entirety of Freaky Nicky’s downward spiral in the movie.

Perhaps the most telling example of this is in a scene which was dropped from the final movie. We get the following interaction between the two in the middle of making out:

<Script>
  <Action>
    Nicky locks eyes with Bear, her expression shifting to SHEER PANIC. With a
    frantic shove, she pushes him away and lets out a BLOOD-CURDLING SCREAM, her
    body trembling.
  </Action>
  <Action>
    Bear remains unfazed, his eerie calm demeanor chilling. Within moments,
    she's back, kissing his stomach. The disturbance registers as nothing more
    than a fleeting inconvenience to him.
  </Action>
</Script>

Bear’s apathy reveals something even more sinister: his absolute, suffocating selfishness. Nicky isn’t a person to him anymore. All she represents is an idealistic relationship, which the movie makes clear through the upbeat romantic montage after he tells her that he likes her. As long as he’s receiving romantic validation from her, it doesn’t matter to him that her identity is completely being eroded - as long as she will _“just be Nicky”_.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/15.jpg"
  alt="Bear's expression after telling Freaky Nicky to just be like Nicky - detached and unbothered."
  caption="Bear knows that the person in front of him isn't the real Nicky - he just doesn't care."
/>

It is also worth noting what it actually takes for Bear to attempt to break the wish. It is not Nicky's suffering, nor the horror of what is being done to her. Bear only moves to intervene when the consequences begin to bleed outward and his social safety net is threatened. The moment his actions begin to cost him something, that is when he panics. And even then, in his attempt to set things right, the film goes out of its way to remind us what a coward he truly is.

In the original script, Bear goes through with his decision to free Nicky from the wish. In the final cut, however, this was changed at the suggestion of Michael Johnston, the actor behind Bear, who approached Curry Barker during filming with the argument that his character simply would not have the stomach to go through with it; Bear is a character defined by cowardice, so why would his final act be any different? This change is significant as it frames Bear as a coward until the very end. He spends the entirety of the film evading accountability for the destruction his selfishness has wrought, and yet, in an almost poetic twist of fate, it is his own wish turned against him that ensures he cannot escape the consequences of it.

In fact, Curry Barker remained steadfast with his portrayal of Bear as a morally reprehensible character throughout the writing process. In an interview with Hollywood Reporter, he revealed that he was offered over 2 million dollars to rewrite the entire script to make Bear a hero.

<Quote>
  “I’m not rewriting the script to make Bear a guy that does all the right
  things. It’s so much more interesting that he doesn’t do the right thing and
  instead tries to make [the relationship] work and he just keeps making bad
  decision after bad decision”.
</Quote>

Ultimately, Bear exists as a deconstruction of the contemporary “nice guy” archetype - the idea of the self-romanticised hopeless dreamer who “just wants a chance”. _Obsession_ breaks down this facade and reveals the underlying sense of entitlement beneath. Which brings us back to the One Wish Willow. It is my belief that the One Wish Willow doesn't grant what we consciously ask for - it grants what we actually want, filtered through the parts of ourselves we don't examine too closely. When Bear is told by the voice on the phone that "he chose this for her," it isn't an admission that Bear wished for the _experience_ of being wanted by her - the shape of a relationship rather than the person to be a part of it.

<ImageWithCaption
  src="/src/assets/films/obsession/16.jpg"
  alt="The One Wish Willow box, the object at the centre of the film's premise."
  caption="The One Wish Willow, and its intricacies, remain unaddressed as one of the movie's many mysteries."
/>

This is what reshapes the entire identity of the film. _Obsession_ adopts the framing of a supernatural thriller, but the monster is not Freaky Nicky. Strip away the esoteric mechanics of the One Wish Willow, and what remains is the horrifying reality of what happens when one person's deep, unexamined desires completely overwrite another human being's existence. However, to fully understand what type of film _Obsession_ stands as, one paramount change between the original script and the final cut remains to be discussed. The original plan was that neither Nicky nor Bear would survive the events of the movie. The script remarks, on the final shot of the film:

<Script>
  <Action>
    We remain fixed on this shot, capturing the blood slowly dripping, a
    haunting and somber testament to this tragic love story.
  </Action>
</Script>

However, that's not what happens. In the final movie, we see the true Nicky emerge one final time. Yet, this isn't a happy ending; it’s the beginning of a fate far crueler. Nicky is left entirely alone in the wreckage of Bear's selfishness. On a practical level, her life is over. But even if she somehow avoided that fate, she has to carry the psychological trauma of this experience forever. At the end of _Obsession_, Nicky has absolutely no one. Her friends are gone, her autonomy was stolen, and the man who did it to her chickened out of his own sacrifice. And in those final seconds, the movie delivers its cruelest blow. As the camera slowly pulls away from her wailing, we - the audience - literally and metaphorically turn our backs on her. We leave her to face whatever lies in store for her, alone.

# Conclusion

_Obsession_'s greatest triumph is not that it is a horror film. It is a tragedy - one that uses the architecture of supernatural horror to tell a deeply human story about desire, ownership, and the quiet violence of an unexamined selfishness. Freaky Nicky is frightening, certainly; but it is Bear, and what he represents, that lingers.

Notwithstanding, it would be a disservice not to acknowledge just how effective the film is on its own terms as a piece of horror cinema. Curry Barker demonstrates, across every frame, a directorial confidence that feels rare for a film of this scale. I find myself eagerly anticipating whatever he turns his attention to next; on the strength of _Obsession_ alone, he has firmly established himself as one of the most exciting up-and-coming directors.
